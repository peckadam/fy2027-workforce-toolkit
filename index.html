Why Dislocated Worker Funding Must Increase

Four converging factors create unprecedented demand on the Dislocated Worker program:

1. AI-Driven Displacement: Brookings estimates 37.1 million U.S. workers hold occupations in the top quartile of AI exposure. McKinsey estimates 12 million workers may need to change occupations by 2030. The St. Louis Fed finds occupations with higher AI exposure experienced larger unemployment rate increases between 2022-2025.

2. SNAP Work Requirements (OBBBA): Expanded to ages 18-65 and parents with children 7+, effective November 1, 2025. CBO estimates 8.8 million individuals are subject to expanded SNAP work requirements (6.5 million non-dependent adults ages 18-65 and 2.3 million parents with children ages 7 and older) (CBO, August 2025). These individuals must document 80 hours/month — many will turn to workforce boards as the only available qualifying activity infrastructure.

3. Medicaid Work Requirements: Beginning January 1, 2027, CBO estimates 18.5 million Medicaid expansion enrollees ages 19-64 will be subject to 80-hour/month community engagement requirements effective January 1, 2027 (CBO). States must implement compliance verification at application and every six months.

4. Workforce Pell Coordination: Effective July 1, 2026, Pell Grant eligibility expands to short-term training (8-15 weeks). Workforce boards must coordinate with providers, ensure program quality, and deliver wrap-around services.

HR 1 (OBBBA) Impact: Populations Newly Subject to Work Requirements

NEW DEMAND ON THE WORKFORCE SYSTEM

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) created two major new work requirement mandates that dramatically expand the population the workforce system must serve — without providing any additional WIOA funding:

SNAP Work Requirements (Effective November 1, 2025):

CBO estimates 8.8 million individuals are subject to expanded SNAP work requirements: 6.5 million non-dependent adults ages 18-65 and 2.3 million parents with children ages 7 and older (CBO, August 2025, based on SNAP Quality Control data FY2023). Newly subject populations include:

— Non-disabled adults ages 55-64 without dependent children (previously exempt)
— Parents/caregivers whose youngest child is ages 7-17 (age 6 and under remain exempt)
— Veterans, people experiencing homelessness, and former foster youth ages 18-24 (exemptions stripped)
— Waiver threshold raised to 10% unemployment (from ~6%), eliminating area waivers in most districts

All must document 80 hours/month of work, training, or approved activities to maintain benefits.

Medicaid Work Requirements (Effective January 1, 2027):

CBO estimates 18.5 million Medicaid expansion enrollees ages 19-64 will be subject to work requirements annually across 41 expansion states plus DC (CBO).

— Applies to expansion adults ages 19-64 in 41 expansion states + DC + GA and WI waivers
— Requires 80 hours/month of work, education, training, or volunteering
— Verification at application and every 6 months (states may verify more frequently)
— Exempt: parents of children under 13, medically frail, pregnant/postpartum
— Non-compliant individuals barred from ACA Marketplace premium tax credits

The workforce system is the only public infrastructure positioned to connect these millions of newly work-required individuals with qualifying employment and training activities at scale.

Program Descriptions (150-250 words each)

WIOA Adult Program

The WIOA Adult Program helps adults with barriers to employment gain new skills and find in-demand jobs in growth sectors. This funding is critical so that we can work locally with those individuals and connect them with employers. Services include career counseling and labor market information, skills assessments, occupational training aligned with in-demand industries, supportive services such as childcare and transportation assistance, and job placement and retention services. Local workforce development boards deliver these services through a network of America's Job Centers, utilizing partnerships with community colleges, training providers, and local employers. The Adult Program is the primary workforce development mechanism for helping low-income workers, displaced workers, and others facing employment barriers access pathways to self-sufficiency and career advancement. With the expansion of SNAP work requirements under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — now covering ages 18-65 and including parents with children ages 7 and older — the Adult Program is the frontline resource helping newly work-required individuals connect to qualifying activities that meet their federal obligations while building real skills for the labor market.

WIOA Youth Program

The WIOA Youth Program serves the 16-24 population with an emphasis on out-of-school youth. Service strategies, developed by workforce providers, prepare youth for employment and/or post-secondary education through strong linkages between academic and occupational learning. Services include pre-apprenticeship training, paid work experience, occupational skills training, education aligned with in-demand industries, supportive services, mentoring, and comprehensive case management. The local workforce board works directly with employers to overcome barriers and provide employment and training for youth participants. With the launch of Workforce Pell Grants in July 2026, the Youth Program plays a critical coordination role — connecting young adults to newly Pell-eligible short-term training programs, providing career coaching that research shows is essential for student success, and ensuring that training investments lead to stackable credentials aligned with regional labor market demands. These services are delivered locally in the district through a network of America's Job Centers.

WIOA Dislocated Worker Program

The Dislocated Worker program provides information on locally available training programs and other services to assist workers who have been laid off or are about to be laid off. Specialists trained to work with these individuals gather information about their needs and begin to organize the services necessary to help them return to work. These services are delivered locally through the district at America's Job Centers. The Dislocated Worker program faces unprecedented demand driven by AI and automation disrupting industries across every Congressional District. Brookings Institution estimates 37.1 million U.S. workers hold occupations in the top quartile of AI exposure — creating a new and expanding category of displaced workers who need rapid retraining for the AI-era labor market. With the launch of Workforce Pell Grants in July 2026, the Dislocated Worker program must also coordinate access to newly Pell-eligible short-term retraining programs, provide career coaching, and deliver the supportive services essential for successful transitions. The Dislocated Worker program is the primary federal response mechanism for AI-driven displacement and requires increased funding.

Justification Template (500-750 words — Customize with District Data)

Part A: SNAP Work Requirements Impact

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (H.R. 1), signed into law July 4, 2025, dramatically expanded SNAP work requirements effective November 1, 2025. The ABAWD age range was extended from 18-54 to 18-65, and parents with children ages 7 and older are now required to comply. The waiver threshold was raised to 10% unemployment, effectively eliminating area waivers in most communities. These individuals must document 80 hours per month of work, training, or approved work-related activities to maintain SNAP benefits. The Congressional Budget Office estimates 8.8 million individuals are subject to expanded SNAP work requirements nationally: 6.5 million non-dependent adults ages 18-65 and 2.3 million parents with children ages 7 and older (CBO, August 2025, based on SNAP Quality Control data FY2023). In [DISTRICT], an estimated [SNAP-AFFECTED-POPULATION] individuals are subject to these work requirements — including non-disabled adults ages 55-64, parents of school-age children, veterans, people experiencing homelessness, and former foster youth who previously held exemptions. Local workforce development boards and their network of America's Job Centers are the primary public infrastructure available to help these individuals identify and access qualifying activities — including job search assistance, occupational training, volunteer work coordination, and workfare placements. Without adequate WIOA Title I Adult funding, boards cannot expand capacity to meet this federally-created demand, forcing impossible choices between serving newly work-required SNAP populations and maintaining services for other job seekers and employers. Furthermore, the OBBBA shifts SNAP administrative cost-sharing from 50-50 federal-state to 75% state / 25% federal beginning in FY2028, increasing fiscal pressure on states and making efficient coordination between SNAP agencies and workforce boards even more critical.

Part B: Medicaid Work Requirements Impact

Beginning January 1, 2027, Medicaid expansion enrollees ages 19-64 must demonstrate 80 hours per month of community engagement activities including employment, job training, education, or volunteering. The Congressional Budget Office estimates 18.5 million Medicaid expansion enrollees ages 19-64 will be subject to these work requirements annually across 41 expansion states plus DC (CBO). Verification is required at application and every six months, with states permitted to verify more frequently. In [DISTRICT], an estimated [MEDICAID-AFFECTED-ADULTS] adults are enrolled through Medicaid expansion and will be subject to these requirements. Exemptions apply to parents of children under 13, medically frail individuals, and pregnant/postpartum individuals. HHS must issue guidance by June 1, 2026, leaving states just seven months to build administrative infrastructure. The public workforce system — through local boards and one-stop career centers — is the natural and essential partner to help individuals meet these requirements through: employment services and job placement; skills training and credentialing; activity tracking and compliance documentation coordination with state Medicaid agencies; and career coaching to move individuals toward self-sufficiency. Evidence from Arkansas's 2018-2019 pilot demonstrates the critical importance of adequate workforce infrastructure: over 18,000 people were unable to navigate the reporting system despite the majority actually working. Adequately funded workforce boards can serve as navigators, helping individuals document compliance while genuinely connecting them to employment — a win for individuals, employers, and the healthcare system.

Part C: AI and Automation Workforce Impacts (Dislocated Worker Justification)

Artificial intelligence and automation are transforming the American economy at an accelerating pace. Brookings Institution estimates 37.1 million U.S. workers hold occupations in the top quartile of AI exposure (Brookings, 2025). In [DISTRICT], approximately [AI-WORKERS] workers hold occupations classified as highly exposed to AI-driven automation, based on Treasury Department analysis of occupational employment data. The St. Louis Federal Reserve has documented that occupations with higher AI exposure experienced significantly larger unemployment rate increases between 2022-2025, confirming that AI-driven job displacement is already underway. This disruption demands increased Dislocated Worker funding for four reasons: 1. SHIFTING DISLOCATED WORKER POPULATION: Unlike previous technological disruptions that primarily affected manufacturing, generative AI targets cognitive tasks performed by knowledge workers — expanding displacement across white-collar and service occupations simultaneously. McKinsey estimates 12 million U.S. workers may need to change occupations by 2030. 2. INCUMBENT WORKFORCE RESKILLING: Workers need evolving skills as AI transforms their current jobs. Local boards partner with employers to deliver upskilling that keeps workers employed and businesses competitive. 3. WORKFORCE PELL COORDINATION: With Workforce Pell launching July 2026, dislocated workers gain new access to Pell-eligible short-term retraining programs. Workforce boards must coordinate with providers, ensure program quality, and deliver the career coaching and supportive services that drive successful outcomes. 4. AI-ENABLED WORKFORCE TOOLS: The workforce development system itself must deploy AI-powered tools that empower Career Coaches with real-time labor market data, career pathway mapping, and personalized career guidance. This requires technology investment at the local level. [DISTRICT] respectfully requests FY2027 Dislocated Worker funding of $1,500,000,000 (up from $1,095,553,000 enacted in FY2026) to enable rapid response capacity and proactive retraining for the AI-driven workforce transformation.

Part D: Workforce Pell Grant Coordination

Effective July 1, 2026, Pell Grant eligibility expands to qualifying short-term training programs (8-15 weeks in length) under the Workforce Pell provisions of the OBBBA. This landmark expansion creates the opportunity to connect more job seekers to affordable, industry-aligned training and portable credentials. However, research consistently demonstrates that financial access alone is insufficient — career coaching, labor market navigation, and wrap-around supports are essential for student success and positive employment outcomes. Local workforce development boards are uniquely positioned to provide this coordination infrastructure. Boards must: identify and connect eligible participants to Pell-eligible training programs; ensure programs meet quality criteria including the 70% completion threshold and positive earnings outcomes; provide real-time career pathway information and labor market intelligence; deliver supportive services (career coaching, childcare, transportation) that improve persistence and completion; and coordinate with community colleges and training providers to align programs with regional employer demand. WIOA Title I funding is the engine that powers this coordination capacity. Without adequate funding, the promise of Workforce Pell risks becoming a poorly coordinated expansion that fails to deliver returns for students, employers, or taxpayers.

Part E: The Funding Ask

[BOARD-NAME] respectfully requests that the Appropriations Committee approve FY2027 funding for WIOA Title I programs under the Department of Labor's Training and Employment Services account at the following levels: • Adult Employment and Training Activities: $1,500,000,000 • Youth Activities: $1,500,000,000 • Dislocated Worker Employment and Training Activities: $1,500,000,000 This represents a necessary increase across all three programs, driven by: (1) Accelerating AI and automation displacement across all industries and skill levels; (2) New SNAP work requirements expanding the population requiring workforce services; (3) Medicaid work requirements (effective January 1, 2027) creating additional demand for employment services and activity verification; and (4) Workforce Pell implementation requiring coordination infrastructure at the local level. The workforce development system cannot simultaneously absorb these new federally-mandated responsibilities AND maintain current service levels without increased investment. Congress created these new demands through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — Congress must also fund the system that will deliver on those mandates. Action Checklist

  • 1. Identify Your Representative(s): Use the District Lookup tab to confirm which district(s) your board serves and the current Representative.
  • 2. Locate the Request Form: Visit your Representative's website — look for "FY27 Appropriations Request" under the "Services" tab. Example: mcdonaldrivet.house.gov/services/fy27-appropriations-requests
  • 3. Prepare Your Request: Use the Advocacy Templates and Customization Tool to draft your program descriptions (150-250 words) and justification (500-750 words).
  • 4. Submit by March 6: Complete and submit your request through your Representative's online form or designated email.
  • 5. Coordinate with Other Offices: If your board serves multiple districts, submit to each Representative. Note other offices on each form — it demonstrates coordinated support.
  • 6. Follow Up with DC Staff: Within 3-5 business days, call the DC office and ask for the Labor/HHS appropriations staffer. Confirm receipt and offer to provide additional information.
  • 7. Report Back: Track your submissions and share with your state workforce association and Chris Andresen for national coordination tracking.
  • 8. Prepare for Possible Hearing Testimony: Some subcommittees invite testimony. Have a 2-3 minute oral version of your justification ready.
